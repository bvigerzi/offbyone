Ludwig Wittgenstein wrote that the limits of my language mean the limits of my world. Programmers understand this intuitively. Choose Python and you think in one way. Choose Haskell and the shape of your thoughts changes entirely.

But it goes deeper than syntax. The abstractions a language offers — or refuses to offer — determine which problems feel easy and which feel impossible.

## The Sapir-Whorf of code

Consider error handling. In Go, errors are values you check. In Rust, they're types you must match. In JavaScript, they're exceptions you might catch, or might not. Each approach doesn't just solve the problem differently — it changes what you consider a problem in the first place.

## Natural language, same story

The same principle applies to human languages. Languages with evidentiality markers force speakers to consider the source of their knowledge with every sentence. Languages without a future tense correlate with higher savings rates. The medium shapes the message, always.

We are, in the end, creatures of our grammars.
