There's a common misconception that creativity requires freedom from constraints. That structure is the enemy of originality. But anyone who has stared at a blank page with the instruction "write anything" knows the truth: total freedom is paralyzing.

Constraints give us edges to push against. A sonnet's fourteen lines aren't a cage — they're a scaffold. The haiku's 5-7-5 isn't a limitation — it's a lens that forces precision.

## Programming as structured thought

Programming is, at its core, the practice of structuring thought so precisely that a machine can follow it. This is why it changes how you think even when you're away from the keyboard. You start noticing ambiguity everywhere. You start breaking problems into smaller pieces instinctively.

## The paradox of choice

Barry Schwartz documented this well: more options lead to worse decisions and less satisfaction. Constraints reduce the search space. They let you go deep instead of wide.

The best writing, the best code, the best designs — they all emerge from well-chosen constraints. The art is in choosing which constraints to embrace.
